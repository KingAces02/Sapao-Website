PAGASA-DOST Weather Radar Station

The PAG-ASA radar weather station was built in Sapao primarily because of its proximity to a mountainous area. Radar stations are typically installed on elevated locations like mountains to minimize obstructions that can interfere with signal transmission, such as buildings or trees. If the station were constructed in a lower, more populated area like the town center, it would be more vulnerable to damage from debris and structural obstacles, especially during strong weather events. Building it on a mountain doesn’t guarantee complete protection, but it significantly reduces the risk of structural damage and allows for better radar coverage. When was the PAG-ASA station built in Sapao? While there is no exact record of the construction date, it is estimated that the PAG-ASA radar station in Sapao was established around 1967 or 1968. However, it officially became fully operational in the early 1970s.

PAG-ASA provides crucial meteorological services, primarily weather forecasting and monitoring. These include daily weather updates, typhoon alerts, rainfall advisories, and climate-related information that help residents, farmers, fisherfolk, and local authorities make informed decisions for safety and planning. How do people in the barangay receive weather updates or warnings from PAG-ASA? Currently, weather updates and warnings from PAG-ASA are disseminated through social media platforms such as Facebook. They also use SMS text messages or Messenger to send alerts directly to individuals or community groups. These methods ensure timely and widespread communication of important weather information.

The PAG-ASA office has been very helpful, especially during typhoons and other natural disasters. For example, during Super Typhoon Yolanda (Haiyan), weather updates from PAG-ASA played a critical role in helping the community prepare in advance. Their forecasts and warnings can save lives by giving people enough time to evacuate or take necessary precautions. Do you think the community understands PAG-ASA’s standards and advisories? For the most part, yes. There are times when the Local Government Unit (LGU) conducts disaster preparedness seminars where PAG-ASA advisories and warning systems are explained. However, these seminars are often conducted by just one speaker and not always directly by PAG-ASA personnel. While helpful, more frequent and inclusive training could improve overall community understanding. Some tourists often ask about the large round object near the station. What is it and what is its purpose? The large round structure is called a radome or radar dome. It houses and protects the radar antenna inside. The antenna spins continuously from 0 to 360 degrees, scanning the skies to monitor cloud patterns, rainfall, and other weather conditions. The dome protects the equipment from harsh weather while still allowing it to function effectively.
